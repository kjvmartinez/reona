Efficiency of Rainwater Harvesting (RWH) as a Flood Control Measure

Summary: An analysis of decentralized rainwater harvesting systems and their quantitative impact on urban stormwater runoff and peak flow reduction.

1. Key Efficiency Metrics (2024–2026 Data)

Research indicates that RWH systems are most effective when "active demand" (using the water for toilets or irrigation) keeps tanks empty enough to capture the next storm's "first flush."

2. Variables Affecting Efficiency

  • Retention Capacity: The "dead storage" available before a storm hits.
  • Adoption Rate: Total efficiency scales linearly; a 50% community adoption rate can reduce local flash flood heights by approximately 0.2m to 0.4m.
  • Catchment Surface: Efficiency is highest on non-permeable surfaces like metal or concrete roofs where runoff is nearly 90% of total rainfall.

3. Conclusion

While RWH is not a substitute for massive drainage infrastructure, it significantly lowers the "Peak Flow" in urban environments. This delay in water entering the sewer system prevents the "Flash Flood" effect commonly seen in paved urban centers.
