Landscape Ecology Analysis of Barangay Bacungan
Puerto Princesa City, Palawan, Philippines

Including Camera Trap Siting Recommendations and BACI Study Design in Relation to Large-Scale Development

1. Overview of Barangay Bacungan’s Landscape Ecology

Barangay Bacungan is a rural coastal barangay located approximately 15–20 km north of Puerto Princesa City center. It features a diverse mosaic of habitats:

  • Low-lying flat alluvial plains suitable for agriculture
  • Extensive mangrove forests (\~379 hectares, dominated by Rhizophora species) along the Bacungan River and coastal margins
  • Riverine and riparian corridors of the Bacungan River (a significant contributor to the city’s watershed)
  • Coastal beaches and dune systems, including Sitio Nagtabon and Sitio Tagkawayan
  • Upland forested hills transitioning toward the Victoria-Anepahan Mountain Range

These interconnected systems support high regional endemism typical of Palawan, a biodiversity hotspot. Community-Based Forest Management (CBFM) agreements and low-impact ecotourism (mangrove river tours, beach visits) are active. Key pressures include quarrying, agricultural runoff, erosion on steeper slopes, and potential habitat fragmentation.

2. Recommended Camera Trap Siting Strategies

Camera traps provide a non-invasive, cost-effective method for monitoring wildlife across Bacungan’s habitat gradient. A stratified sampling design is recommended:

Upland Forest and Hilly Transitions

Place cameras along game trails, ridge lines, water sources, and fruiting trees. Use ground-level (30–60 cm) and arboreal mounts for species such as Palawan leopard cat, binturong, pangolin, civets, and porcupines.

Riverine and Riparian Zones

Target riverbanks, shallow crossings, and riparian vegetation to monitor semi-aquatic species, waterbirds, and movement corridors.

Mangrove and Estuarine Areas

Install on elevated platforms, stable prop roots, or boardwalks to avoid tidal flooding. Focus on landward edges and channels.

Coastal Beaches and Ecotones (including Sitio Tagkawayan)

Position along dune vegetation and forest edges to capture beach users, birds, and terrestrial-coastal transitions.

Lowland Agricultural Edges

Monitor forest-farm boundaries to assess edge effects and human-wildlife interactions.

Best Practices: 20–50+ cameras in a stratified grid; multi-season deployments (wet/dry); record covariates (habitat type, distance to edge, human activity); involve local CBFM cooperatives for maintenance.

3. The SM Corporation 500-hectare Acquisition in Sitio Tagkawayan

Sitio Tagkawayan features scenic beachfront with beach-forest transitions, potential mangrove/riverine linkages, and proximity to the Bacungan River system. A large-scale acquisition (\~500 hectares or 5 km²) in this area could span multiple habitat types, introducing risks of:

  • Direct habitat loss and fragmentation of coastal, mangrove, and upland areas
  • Altered hydrology and increased sedimentation affecting the river and mangroves
  • Heightened human disturbance, light pollution, and edge effects
  • Changes in wildlife movement corridors between uplands and coast

Balanced development could bring economic benefits (tourism infrastructure, employment) if it incorporates sustainable design elements that complement existing low-impact ecotourism and CBFM initiatives.

4. BACI Study Design for Monitoring Development Impacts

Before-After-Control-Impact (BACI) is a robust quasi-experimental design widely used in environmental science to evaluate the effects of large developments.

Component Description
Impact Site The \~500-hectare property in Sitio Tagkawayan and immediate buffer zones
Control Sites Matched unaffected habitats elsewhere in Bacungan (e.g., Sitio Nagtabon, Candis Falls areas)
Before Phase Baseline data collected prior to construction or major land clearing
After Phase Repeated monitoring during construction and operational phases

The key statistical test examines the interaction between Time (Before vs. After) and Treatment (Impact vs. Control). This isolates the development’s effect from natural seasonal or yearly variation.

Application to Camera Traps: Deploy stratified camera arrays in both impact and control zones immediately to capture true “Before” data. Monitor occupancy, detection rates, activity patterns, and community metrics of key species (mammals, birds, reptiles). Use GLMMs or Bayesian models for analysis.

Strengths: Strong causal inference. Limitations: Requires sufficient pre-impact sampling; best supplemented with multiple controls and other survey methods.

Recommendations

  • Initiate baseline camera trapping as soon as possible for robust BACI implementation
  • Preserve mangrove and riparian buffers (minimum 20–50+ meters)
  • Incorporate wildlife corridors, reduced lighting, and green infrastructure in any development plans
  • Engage local stakeholders (barangay officials, CBFM cooperatives, City ENRO, PCSDS) for co-management and permitting
  • Integrate monitoring results into the Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) and adaptive management
